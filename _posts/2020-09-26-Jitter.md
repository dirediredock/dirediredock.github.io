<br>

Shown here is a histogram of the elevations per pixel from the surface interpolator described in post #005, for the whole basin (including mountain peaks) of Burrard Inlet. Surprisingly, the most common elevation 0m, or sea level at low tide.

<br>
![](/images/ElevationJitter.png)
<br>

A special implementation of the surface interpolator can use non-lattice points over which the kernel moves, for example, to study elevation variance of specific features. In this case, points are taken from four different shoreline contours of Burrard Inlet, one for the high water mark and three for elevation depths, at 0m, 2m and 5m underwater. All of these contours are shapefiles created by the Canadian Hydrographic Service.

<br>
![](/images/JitterLine1.png)
<br>

Let's explore the high water mark first. Shown here in blue is the deconstruction of the shoreline starting at Point Atkinson and closing at Point Gray. In red is the running mean, so the general trend is visualized more clearly. Even though the median high water mark lies at 4.59m above sea level, the amount of variance is staggering, with the mean at 3.67m with a standard deviation of ±5.26m. This strong anomaly, or jitter, in elevation is likely due to the nature of the high water mark, it is not based on an elevation, but on the visual limit of water at high tide. 

<br>
![](/images/JitterLine2.png)
<br>

Here the high water mark (yellow) is compared to contours at 0m depth (green), 2m depth (blue), and 5m depth (purple). Each has three running means at different levels of coarseness, to highlight the elevation jitter. The contour for 0m, or sea level, has its median at -0.35m, mean at -0.44m, with a standard deviation of ±2.54m. This means that the line that delimits the low tide has an error of almost 3 meters, which is quite troublesome. Next is the contour at 2m depth, with median at -2.23m, mean at -2.41m, with a standard deviation of ±1.39m. This hints that deeper shorelines, extreme low tides, have more consistent profiles and less jitter error given how the standard deviation decreases as depth increases. Finally, the perpetually underwater contour at 5m depth has a median at -5.11m, mean at -5.22, and standard deviation at ±1.36m.

<br>
![](/images/Jitter_Histogram.png)
<br>

An alternative visualization of the high water mark and depth contours using histograms. Perhaps elevation anomaly decreases with depth because the upper layers are much more energetic and weather-driven. This high energy, coupled with the capricious geometry of a fjord basin, can create localized clusters of tidal waters well above sea level, that are marked as high water anyway. This phenomenon is important to consider when addressing shoreline change, like what is expected under climate change and global sea level rise.
